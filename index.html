What time is it really where you are?

India spans 2,900 km east to west — roughly the same as the US from coast to coast. Yet the entire country runs on a single timezone: IST. In Arunachal Pradesh, the sun rises over an hour earlier than in Gujarat. China is even more extreme — one timezone for a country that should span five.

Your clock says 9 AM. The sun disagrees.

That thought stuck with me. So I built something about it.

An idea, a conversation, a product

I had a clear idea, but still just an idea. No wireframes, no design brief, no sprint planning. I sat down with AI and described what I wanted: a dark, celestial-themed interface, search-driven location input, a sun arc showing the sun’s position, a gauge comparing solar vs official time.

What happened next still catches me off guard:

  • Components built with design details I hadn’t even specified — the star field background, the gold gradient on the sun arc, the instrument-gauge diff bar
  • Responsive layouts across breakpoints I didn’t enumerate
  • Accessibility — ARIA roles, keyboard navigation on the search dropdown

All of this from a conversation. From a thought.

I’m not saying AI replaced engineering judgment. I made decisions, reviewed output, asked for changes, steered the direction. But the distance between “what if” and “here it is, deployed and working” compressed from weeks to hours.

That’s the shift I think people are still underestimating. Not that AI writes code — that’s the least interesting part. It’s that the barrier between having an idea and holding a working product has dropped to nearly zero.

The question is no longer “can I build this?” It’s “is this idea worth building?”
